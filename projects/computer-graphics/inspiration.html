Inspiration

When I was younger, I was always trying to learn how things worked. This interest lead me to take up programming, because I wanted to understand how video games were made. Since then, I have become more interested in machine learning and how to create self changing systems. Eventually, I stumbled across a video, by Matt Parker, about an AI that was made from match boxes and beads. Fascinated, I decided that I needed to make one of my own.

[1] MENACE: the pile of matchboxes which can learn.

What is MENACE?

MENACE is a match box computer thought of and created by Donald Michie in 1961[2]. He did this in order to demonstrate adaptive learning was possible in computer systems. Matchboxes were used due to not having access to a computer at the time[2].

How Does it Work?

There are 304 match boxes. Each box has a picture on the face that represents a board position. Inside of each box are colored beads that when randomly chosen represent the move that MENACE wants to make - each color corresponds to a square on the board. You play tic-tac-toe with MENACE by picking the box that matches the current state of the board, randomly selecting a bead, and making that move. Then on your turn you play as normal. Go back and forth between MENACE and you making moves until the game is either won, lost, or drawn. MENACE learns between games, because if MENACE wins, you put the beads back into their respective boxes with three extra of the same color. This makes MENACE more likely to make those same moves in the future. When MENACE draws, you just put back the beads into the box. This keeps MENACE playing the same. And when MENACE loses, you remove the beads that made those decisions from the boxes. This makes MENACE less likely to make those moves in the future. As a result, MENACE gets more likely to make good moves after each game resulting in a win or loss. Over time MENACE will go from playing randomly to expert level play.

More Information

The method of learning that is taking place is called reinforcement learning, in that we are using punishments and rewards in order to steer the machines behavior in the direction that we want. This is commonly used for training neural networks.

Making your own

If you want to make your own, you can follow along by reading through the rest of the project. In all honesty, it is a good bit of work to complete, so if you have young kids or no programming experience maybe try a simplified version for the game "hexapawn". That's what I did after watching the video for the first time, and while it is less impressive it is still fun to show off to friends.
